Abstract

Long-horizon humanoid-object interaction (HOI) is a fundamental challenge for embodied agents, requiring both semantic understanding and physical feasibility. While recent generative approaches have shown impressive capabilities in synthesizing plausible HOI from language and scene context, they often suffer from unrealistic object contacts and incorrect physical motion due to a lack of physical simulation. Meanwhile, simulation-based HOI methods typically rely on motion capture demonstrations to train task-specific policies, which limits their ability to scale to long-horizon interactions involving diverse and compositional goals. To address this, we introduce \ourName, a unified framework that tightly couples a conditional diffusion model with a physics-based humanoid tracker. Our method first generates interaction trajectories conditioned on language, object geometry, and sparse waypoints. Then, a simulated humanoid robot refines the trajectory via contact-aware policy tracking, and the resulting humanoid-object states are fed back into the generative process for the next phase. We also introduce an automatic keyframe extraction strategy to reduce the redundancy of the generative model. Experiments demonstrate that our approach significantly improves interaction robustness and long-term capability, achieving high success rates across diverse manipulation tasks.

Method

(a) HOI generation conditioned on language and object; (b) learning a universal human-object interaction (HOI) policy; (c) closed-loop inference and control with physical simulation. SimGenHOI uses a diffusion model for keyframe generation and reinforcement learning to train a general HOI policy in the Isaac Gym simulator.
